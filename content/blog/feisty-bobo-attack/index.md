Bobo only occasionally revealed the instincts of an actual cat. Sometimes I wondered whether living alone with me made him lonely, or whether his understanding of the world was missing something that other cats naturally had. He didn’t groom himself, and he certainly never coughed up hairballs.

Fortunately, I eventually discovered that his hunting instinct was alive and well—even if neither his eyesight nor his sense of smell seemed particularly reliable. If a piece of kibble fell onto a section of floor that happened to be the same color, it was as good as gone.

Until he completely lost sight of it, however, Bobo never gave up. He would stand on his hind legs, leap into the air, and reach out with both paws, determined to capture that single bite of food.

That kind of persistence would have made him very well suited to a PhD and a career in research. His forgetful brain would have helped too. Sometimes he would lose track of a piece of kibble, turn around, and discover it again as though he had just won the lottery.

With instincts and an attitude like that, why was I the one doing the PhD instead of him?

Later, I realized that perhaps Bobo’s nose wasn’t quite as unreliable as I had thought. Whenever I ate fish, drank milk, or sliced butter, he immediately launched himself into pursuit.

Even when I tried to move around as quietly as possible, he would somehow appear behind me without warning and scare me half to death. Eventually, I accepted that none of my activities could escape Bobo’s supervision.

Fortunately, he never learned how to use a straw. Otherwise, he would have been entirely capable of claiming a whole bottle of milk for himself.

If his body had allowed it, I would have happily shared everything I had with him. I often placed myself in a strange, self-appointed role: I wanted Bobo to experience the world through my eyes because I worried that his life as a cat might otherwise be incomplete.

In reality, Bobo was probably much freer and happier than I was. I was clearly the one overthinking things.

Trimming Bobo’s claws was never difficult. I often saw people online explaining that they had to place their cats inside boxes, cut holes in the sides, and pull out one paw at a time just to avoid being bitten.

Bobo never required that kind of negotiation. He would sit quietly on the sofa while I gently pressed out each claw and trimmed them one by one.

I like to say that, in this photograph, Bobo had developed a personal grudge against the nail file. The truth was simply that he didn’t have enough toys.

During his first year or two, I was apparently too lazy even to buy him a proper cat wand or scratching board. So he scratched the sofa. Then I covered the entire sofa with a blanket.

Why was I willing to buy an enormous blanket instead of a scratching board? Because I knew he would scratch the sofa anyway—just as he would always insist on drinking water from my cup instead of his own.

When Bobo was little, he rarely caused much trouble. The worst thing he usually did was deliberately knock my erasers onto the floor and bat them around until they disappeared.

Eventually, all my erasers were gone. By then, I had also finished the coursework portion of my PhD and no longer needed pencils and erasers for homework. Even if Bobo wanted to play with one, there were none left to steal.

His toilet-paper rampage only happened once. Secretly, I was almost happy about it because it seemed as though he had finally discovered something new and entertaining.

I still scolded him because I didn’t want to encourage the habit. But honestly, as long as something wasn’t poisonous or dangerous to his health, I didn’t particularly mind how much mischief he made with it.

Most of the time, Bobo was simply clingy. Occasionally, however, his desire for physical closeness became so urgent that he would launch himself from the floor like a rocket and land directly on my lap while I was sitting in a chair.

I had no objection. He never missed—not even once. The accuracy of his landings always amazed me. Bobo seemed able to reach any place he decided to go, with the kitchen stove being the only exception.

At other times, Bobo would forcefully rub his bald little head against my face. Cats are supposed to do this as an expression of affection.

If another cat’s love was like a wave rolling gently onto the shore, Bobo’s love was a tsunami.

If we had been characters in a cartoon, I’m fairly certain our faces would have ended up permanently stuck together.
