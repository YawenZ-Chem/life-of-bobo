When I first picked up Bobo, the thing I remembered most was his enormous voice. In the freezing weather between New York and Boston, his cries were powerful enough to make my eardrums vibrate.

Once we arrived home, all I could think about was how tiny he was. In the middle of the apartment’s living room, he barely seemed to take up any space. Then, after following me everywhere for a while, clingy little Bobo climbed onto my shoulder, and we took our first photograph together.

That was when I noticed how enormous his head was compared with the rest of him. His tiny body and oversized head gave him the proportions of a cartoon character. He narrowed his eyes at the camera with an expression of unmistakable disdain, even though the rest of him was still clearly kitten-sized.

If Bobo had appeared in a cartoon, there was no question that he would have been the main character.

As Bobo grew, he gradually discovered new ways to stay physically attached to me. Sleeping cheek-to-cheek was only the beginning.

Whenever I lay on my stomach to use my phone, he would climb onto my back like the final piece of a human-and-cat pyramid. His head fit perfectly against my shoulder, and his purring poured directly into my ear. It was probably the most comforting and relaxing sound in the world.

Bobo and I also developed an unspoken understanding whenever a camera appeared. Somehow, we would look toward the lens at exactly the same moment. Over time, even our expressions began to synchronize. We looked less like two roommates caught in a casual photograph and more like a pair deliberately selected to pose together.

As Bobo grew older, the way he narrowed his eyes at the camera became even more pronounced. Combined with his triangular head and enormous triangular ears, his face had a kind of geometric symmetry that I found strangely beautiful.

A few centuries ago, his appearance might have been used to represent something ominous—perhaps a tiny demon. My own knowledge of demons comes almost entirely from emojis, so I can hardly claim expertise. Still, the image suited him precisely because it contrasted so completely with his actual personality.

He might have looked like a small creature of darkness, but underneath that dramatic face was a gentle, clingy roommate. That contradiction was one of the things I loved most about him.

Most of our photographs were taken after Bobo jumped onto my desk. It was difficult to find a pose that placed our heads at the same height, and I never believed that he particularly enjoyed having his picture taken.

Perhaps Bobo believed that memories belonged inside the body. His memories were probably made from sounds, smells, touch, and warmth. Visual records may have meant nothing to him. In that respect, I envied him.

Unfortunately, I was only a clumsy human. I did not trust my nose, ears, and memory to preserve everything I wanted to keep. I needed to write things down and take photographs to compensate for my inability to hold on to time.

Fortunately, Bobo was patient. Whenever he climbed onto my desk and I saw the two of us reflected together, I wanted to preserve the moment.

Bobo was also slightly restless—or perhaps he simply did not understand what a photograph was. He did not know where the camera was or where he was expected to look. If I called his name, he would usually lift his head and look at me instead.

With enough communication, however, we could eventually produce a good picture together. I understood cameras and posing, while Bobo contributed a naturally photogenic little face.

After enough practice, Bobo seemed to become more comfortable with our photographs. Or perhaps we had simply spent so much time together that he had become skilled at finding somewhere to sit beside me.

My back was one of his favorite locations. Whenever I lay down, he would walk onto me, settle into position, and look toward the camera as though this had always been a perfectly ordinary portrait arrangement.

That was how we unlocked another official roommate pose.

{{< /story-row >}}

When I say that our expressions gradually became alike, the truth is that I was probably the one learning to imitate Bobo.

Bobo’s face did not have many available settings. He had no eyebrows, and he could not smile by stretching the corners of his mouth. If his eyes were wide and round, he looked innocent, clear-minded, and slightly foolish. If he narrowed them into thin lines, he immediately transformed into a professional model.

Those were essentially his two expressions.

Eventually, I began narrowing my eyes in photographs too. Unfortunately, Bobo looked fashionable because he had a naturally small head and a tiny face—perfect for the camera. My own larger face continued to occupy considerably more of the frame.

Same expression, perhaps. Different results.

{{< /story-row >}}

To keep my face from resembling a full Mid-Autumn Festival moon, I eventually learned to hide part of my head behind Bobo’s. It was an elegant solution to the problem.

This salmon dinner photograph became one of my favorite pictures of us. The colors and our expressions had exactly the dark, dramatic quality I wanted.

It also revealed something important: when Bobo’s face appeared in front of mine, he became the unquestionable star of the image. If this had been a movie poster, his salary would have been several times larger than mine.

On the evening the photograph was taken, Bobo’s actual payment consisted of two pieces of salmon.

My little bald roommate was clearly born to be a star. That kind of style cannot be taught.
