Bobo has flourished through a lifetime of strange poses and inexplicable decisions. I often find myself wondering how he ranks the important things in his life.

Food appears to occupy first place. Whenever I see other cats being taken to the hospital because they are picky eaters or refuse to drink enough water—or watch their roommates coax them through every meal—I feel especially grateful. Bobo approaches every kind of food with complete dedication, drinks an impressive amount of water every day, and probably lives a healthier life than I do.

At other times, however, I suspect that I might be his first priority.

No matter how uncomfortable his position looks, Bobo stubbornly insists on staying beside me. I can make space for him on the bed or sofa, but there simply isn’t a designated Bobo seat at my desk.

Day after day, he stretches his neck upward and rests his head against the edge of the desk. His neck looks as though it has been lengthened for a career in ballet, yet he refuses to move somewhere else and lie down comfortably.

Sometimes I raise my leg so his chin has a softer place to rest. This relieves Bobo’s suffering by transferring it directly to my leg. For the sake of his neck, I occasionally grit my teeth and hold the position.

Then I look at him resting his head contentedly against the desk and suddenly feel a little sad. Why are both of us so stubborn, and what exactly are we trying to prove?

Beyond his inexplicable desk behavior, Bobo watches me during almost every moment of the day. He usually does so without any expression at all.

I often wonder whether he understands the expressions on my face. As a cat without eyebrows or whiskers, does he understand what human eyebrows are trying to communicate?

Every night before going to sleep, I spend far too long looking at my phone. Bobo doesn’t have a phone, so his only available entertainment is staring at my face. If he can interpret my expressions, perhaps this is his way of indirectly scrolling through my phone.

When I lie on my stomach to read, Bobo also insists on participating. Unfortunately, I don’t make many expressions while reading, so he probably cannot absorb Kafka by watching my face.

When I prop myself up on my elbows, my arms and body create a triangular space. This is prime Bobo real estate. He settles directly between my arms, pretends to read along, and mostly interferes with the process.

Bobo’s other secret retreat is the radiator.

I don’t know when he discovered that it produced heat, or why he was willing to remain in such an uncomfortable position for hours. Predictably, I felt sorry for him and bought him an electric heating blanket.

Once again, I had misunderstood the workings of Bobo’s mind. He did not abandon his beloved radiator. As far as he was concerned, the heating blanket simply meant that our home now contained Warm Place Number One and Warm Place Number Two.

I originally bought the ladder so I could change light bulbs. Then I discovered that every appearance of the ladder inspired enormous enthusiasm in Bobo—enough enthusiasm to make any cat tree violently jealous.

I still don’t know whether he wanted to stand somewhere high or simply wanted to bring himself to my eye level. Either way, whenever he saw the ladder, he had to climb it.

His claws couldn’t properly grip its plastic steps. Several times he nearly fell, and I had to catch him. None of this frightened or discouraged him.

I admired his bravery while quietly wondering whether he might also be a little foolish.

{{< /story-row >}}

Eventually, Bobo seemed to understand what the front door meant. I disappeared through it, and sometime later, it returned me to him.

He began standing in front of the door whenever I put on my shoes. I couldn’t tell whether he was reluctant to let me leave or wanted to come with me.

Later, I developed another theory: Bobo wanted to go outside because a little calico girl lived across the hall. Perhaps she was the first love of his cat life.

Of course, he might simply have hated seeing me leave. If Bobo understood that food was free inside our apartment but had to be earned through labor outside, I’m certain he would never think about that door again.

Taking photographs with Bobo at night often produced fascinating results. His glowing eyes made him look like a small demon.

To be fair, even with the lights on, his expressionless face could look slightly demonic. But compared with living alone in a silent apartment and returning every day to an empty home, Demon Bobo gave me a surprising amount of security.

Some of that security was probably superstition. A demon like Bobo could presumably defeat any lesser supernatural threat.

Besides, he loved me.

{{< /story-row >}}

When I took Bobo outside—usually for a veterinary appointment—he transformed into a nervous little creature inside his backpack.

He had no choice but to observe the world around him: cars on the street, people passing by, and announcements echoing through the subway. Eventually, he would begin to yell at full volume.

To me, this sounded exactly like the excited yelling he produced when he saw food. I could not distinguish between the two at all.

Apparently, this was the absolute limit of my talent for learning Bobo’s language.

{{< /story-row >}}

When Bobo was young, he gradually realized that the kitchen stove was the place where food was born.

Unfortunately, he was too small to jump onto it. I don’t know whether he secretly attempted the jump while I was away, but at some point he concluded that it was impossible and began investigating alternative methods.

One day, I caught him trying to scale the kitchen drawers as though they were a ladder.

What an intelligent little Bobo. Why hadn’t this level of problem-solving ability earned him a PhD too?

Although Bobo spent his days climbing, jumping, and investigating cabinets, he was very committed to cleanliness. Every day, he carefully held and licked the various parts of his body.

Occasionally, he also held and licked parts of mine.

He never seemed to consider how much work it would take to clean an enormous creature like me. With that determination to pursue an impossible task despite overwhelming odds, Bobo would undoubtedly have published in a top journal.

One of a cat’s natural instincts is to watch the world through a window, and Bobo was no exception. He watched during the day and at night, in summer and winter.

Unfortunately, our apartment did not offer much of a view when he was young. Outside the window stood the brick building across the courtyard, occasionally interrupted by a few passing swallows. That was about it.

Perhaps this was the cat equivalent of watching television. If so, Bobo’s television probably had only two channels—and one of them showed nothing but advertisements.
