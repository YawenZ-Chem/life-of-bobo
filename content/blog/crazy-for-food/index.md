Bobo’s understanding of the world seemed to consist almost entirely of human affection and an endless procession of food. I was never sure whether this was because he didn’t actually think of himself as a cat.

For a long time, his world contained only the two of us. If he spent every day sitting beside me at the desk and every night sleeping on my pillow, perhaps he simply assumed that he was a smaller version of me. If that was truly what he believed, then all the better.

Bobo’s enthusiasm for food appeared when he was still very small. The carefully measured portion I prepared according to his weight seemed to represent only the tip of his appetite. If allowed to eat without limits, his belly would swell into a little balloon.

He was never foolish enough to eat until he became sick, but once his stomach began looking larger than his head, I bought several slow-feeder dishes. Bobo accepted them without protest. That was how he approached nearly every arrangement: scheduled meals, unusual clothing, and all the other decisions made on his behalf.

{{< /story-row >}}

As a kitten, he also received a carefully measured bowl of goat milk every day. When I served it in a shallow dish, he splashed milk everywhere with his tongue. I eventually gave him a deeper bowl, so he responded by burying his entire head inside it.

Bobo’s love of dairy products was unmistakable. Butter, yogurt, whipped cream, and cake all possessed a fatal attraction. Whenever I left an empty bowl of Greek yogurt nearby, he would rush over, bury his face in it, and polish it until it shone.

I didn’t mind employing him as a dish-cleaning assistant. I simply had to put the bowl into the dishwasher immediately afterward. Otherwise, it looked so clean that I might forget it still needed washing.

Baking a cream cake required a much more serious negotiation. Bobo would circle the kitchen while I repeatedly lifted him down from the table—sometimes what felt like a hundred times. Cake was far too rich and sugary for him, so I had to remain firm.

To his credit, Bobo was often more patient than I was. He would return after every rejection, calmly waiting for another opportunity. Occasionally, he timed his attack perfectly and escaped with a pawful of cream. On those occasions, I had to admit that he had won.

If Bobo truly believed that he and I were the same species, then perhaps he also deserved a little ceremony around his meals. We couldn’t eat exactly the same food, but gathering several of his favorites onto one plate could still create a sense of abundance.

For me, the human equivalent might be hot pot with iced milk tea, followed by a slice of durian crêpe cake. Bobo’s earliest tasting menu was much simpler: canned tuna, boiled shrimp, and one little meat stick.

As he grew older, he gradually sampled more kinds of food. He loved nearly every fish he encountered, making seafood the one place where our diets genuinely overlapped. Whenever I ate fish, I began saving a small piece for him.

Every once in a while, I would gather his favorite things and prepare an unnecessarily elaborate feast.

Bobo had no interest in presentation. From his perspective, arranging the plate and taking photographs only created a cruel delay between himself and dinner. Still, in service of his human roommate’s desire for ceremony, he was forced to wait while I finished taking every picture I wanted.

Only then could he finally begin the meal he had been staring at so intensely.

Occasionally, Bobo’s desire for food overwhelmed his manners completely. He might bat at my chopsticks, reach directly into my cup, or wrap himself around my head and try to lick food from my mouth.

In those moments, everything else in the world disappeared. There was only Bobo and the particular bite he had decided must belong to him.

Yet no matter how excited he became, he never extended his claws far enough to hurt me. I was always impressed that such a small and enthusiastic creature could maintain that much control.

Eating was the one activity that received Bobo’s complete and undivided attention. He approached each meal like a lover arriving for a long-awaited reunion. Sometimes I would lie on the floor simply to watch him eat. When he shoveled up kibble, his mouth moved like a tiny hoe. Bobo could probably have hosted a very successful eating show.

One day, after I had finished some durian, I discovered him licking the container. Apparently, Bobo liked durian.

To let him experience the actual flavor rather than the watery residue left in the box, I saved him the smallest taste. He loved it, exactly as expected. Unfortunately, such a sugary fruit could never become a regular solution to his enormous appetite.

Because Bobo was so remarkably unselective, tube treats rarely appeared in his life. Most of the time, I didn’t even need real food to attract him. I only had to crinkle a plastic bag and pretend that I was eating something.

He would come running without hesitation. After discovering that he had been deceived, he would walk away—only to return immediately when the bag crinkled again.

Perhaps a creature willing to abandon all caution in pursuit of food must live a very happy life. The tube treats in our home certainly weren’t disappearing quickly; some of them were nearly expired before we managed to finish the package.

{{< /story-row >}}

At a little over one year old, Bobo received an unfortunate announcement during his veterinary checkup: he was officially overweight.

The evidence had already become visible at home. When he sat down, his growing belly rested quietly on top of his feet. His dinner was reduced from two spoonfuls to one—a catastrophe of historic proportions in Bobo’s opinion.

I sympathized with the misery of dieting. The difference was that if I became desperately hungry, I could open the refrigerator. Bobo could only charge toward every edible object that crossed his path.

He didn’t normally care for pineapple, but during his diet he suddenly began eating it. He also discovered a stale white steamed bun that had been sitting on the table all day. It had become dry and hard enough to resemble a stone and was already destined for the trash. Bobo attacked it enthusiastically anyway.

Later evidence confirmed that this wasn’t merely an act of desperation. Bobo genuinely liked bread, steamed buns, and nearly every other form of dough.

{{< /story-pair >}}

Bobo wasn’t completely indiscriminate. Durian remained his favorite fruit, but he disliked nearly every other fruit he encountered.

Eventually, I realized that he simply assumed anything I ate was also available to him. Whenever food appeared on my desk, he came over to conduct an inspection. Most items didn’t even require a taste. One serious sniff was enough for him to reject them.

Neither my watermelon juice nor the strawberry managed to win his heart.

{{< /story-pair >}}

Bobo’s careful observation of me extended beyond food. He was fascinated by the water in my glass, as though anything I drank must taste sweeter than the water in his own bowl.

He would force his head as deeply into the glass as possible. Unfortunately, drinking this way also meant lowering his nose dangerously close to the water. He frequently inhaled a little, pulled back to snort, and then immediately put his head into the glass again.

Even when the process became unnecessarily difficult, Bobo remained devoted to drinking my water. Apparently, inconvenience could never defeat determination.
