When Bobo first came home, he was so tiny that I could hold him in one hand. After following me everywhere for a while, he fell asleep on my shoulder.

Whenever he slept, he would begin curled into the shape of a pangolin. Then, once he started dreaming, his body would slowly unfold into all kinds of anatomically questionable positions.

Sometimes he became a warm little ball beside my face, practically steaming with heat. I wondered whether the feeling rising inside me was that legendary glow of maternal love.

But I have always disliked describing Bobo as my child. For a long time, we were roommates who depended on each other. I think the happiness I felt in that moment came from knowing that an animal trusted me completely. The trust and love between a person and an animal may be among the greatest things in the world.

Sometimes I brought my laptop to bed. Working until the very second before falling asleep was a terrible habit, but to Bobo, walking into the bedroom and lying down meant that it was time for bed. Naturally, he would trot in after me.

First, he scratched at the blanket until I lifted it for him. Then he crawled underneath, arranged himself so that as much of his skin as possible was touching mine, and quietly fell into a deep sleep. Not a single step of this ritual could be skipped.

That left me working with one arm around Bobo and the other on my computer. I even wore headphones because I was afraid the sound might wake him. Eventually, however, I discovered that no amount of noise could disturb him—unless I got out of bed.

The moment I sat up, he became instantly alert. Whether I was going to the kitchen or the bathroom, he followed silently behind me. As soon as I returned to bed, he climbed back in, repeated the entire ritual, and immediately fell asleep again.

Bobo’s extraordinary sleeping ability worked just as well at my desk. While I worked, he would drift off without my noticing. Unless I stood up or the automatic feeder released food, almost nothing could wake him.

I knew that cats naturally sleep in fragments throughout the day, but I still admired his ability to fall asleep and wake up whenever he pleased. Perhaps only a creature with such an uncomplicated mind can sleep that deeply. Most people struggle with insomnia—and a few might even catch the insomnia plague from *One Hundred Years of Solitude*.

Through his tireless experimentation, Bobo eventually discovered another way to keep as much of his skin in contact with mine as possible.

Whenever I lay on my stomach to read or use my phone, he climbed onto my back and tucked his head perfectly into the curve of my neck. From there, he could once again fall into a deep and peaceful sleep.

Apparently, physical contact with me had medicinal properties. Any form of contact, from any angle, seemed equally effective.

{{< /story-row >}}

Eventually, I became just as dependent on falling asleep with Bobo in my arms. After holding the same warm little creature every night, it felt no different from sleeping with a perfectly temperature-controlled hot-water bottle.

It wasn’t only his warmth. Bobo also had a peculiar, slightly oily scent that became deeply familiar to me. Later, while traveling, I lay awake in a hotel bed, surrounded by cold sheets, staring at the ceiling until the middle of the night.

That was when I realized that contact with Bobo helped me sleep too.

I had thought I was the medicine. It turned out that I was the one who needed it.
